## Why canonical

Gogh treats Butterick as the typography-law source behind readable measure, scale, line spacing, and number-setting constraints, but this file is limited to the local extracts and ledger. The core value is operational: typography becomes measurable enough for agents to avoid unreadable walls, unstable counters, cramped lines, and loose claims about polish.

## Distilled principles

1. Measure should be evaluated in characters, not just pixels.
2. Body lines around 45-90 characters are the local evidence basis for `max-w-[65ch]` style constraints.
3. A text block that is too wide is a reading problem even if it looks spacious.
4. Body text needs enough size and line spacing to be read without effort.
5. Typography rules should support comprehension before they support brand personality.
6. Headline type and body type can contrast, but the relationship must be deliberate.
7. Tabular figures belong where changing or aligned numbers must not shift the layout.
8. Do not apply tabular figures blindly to every number; use them where stability matters.
9. Preserve readable rhythm through consistent line height, measure, and paragraph spacing.
10. A max-width rule for prose is not decorative. It is a reading constraint.
11. Avoid squeezing long words or labels into narrow UI without wrap, clamp, or resize behavior.
12. Use typography as a system of roles: display, heading, body, label, caption, and numeric data.
13. Treat type defaults as suspect until they have been checked against content length and viewport.
14. Typography quality is part of accessibility, not only visual taste.
