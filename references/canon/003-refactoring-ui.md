## Why canonical

Gogh treats this as a canonical design-principles source, but this file is explicitly limited to what the local extracts and claim ledger record. The durable ideas here are visual hierarchy, deliberate whitespace, and reducing unnecessary borders. These principles explain why many Gogh rules prefer stronger hierarchy, more intentional density, shadows, contrast, and spacing over generic card outlines.

## Distilled principles

1. Visual hierarchy is built from relative size, color, weight, spacing, and placement.
2. Interfaces feel undesigned when everything has equal emphasis.
3. Start from generous whitespace, then remove space deliberately when density has a real job.
4. Density is a design decision, not an accident of running out of room.
5. Use fewer borders when spacing, contrast, background tone, or shadow can communicate grouping.
6. A border should earn its place by clarifying structure, not by decorating every box.
7. Use weight and type scale to signal what should be read first, second, and last.
8. Avoid weak middle-ground hierarchy. A small 1.5x jump often reads timid beside a stronger type-scale move.
9. Treat cards as one grouping tool among many, not the default answer to every section.
10. Let primary content be visibly primary; supporting text should look supportive.
11. Use whitespace to create scanning lanes, not blankness for its own sake.
12. Reserve high-contrast accents for actions, active states, or semantic emphasis.
13. Prefer a small number of strong, repeated hierarchy rules to many one-off tweaks.
14. Review interfaces by squinting at rhythm and contrast before arguing over individual colors.
