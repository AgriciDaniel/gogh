## Why canonical

Kowalski's essay is canonical for Gogh as practitioner doctrine: taste can be explained, decomposed, and packaged for agents. The local evidence also warns against misreading it as a review of Leon Lin's Taste Skill; it is a parallel effort by a sponsor-adjacent practitioner. Gogh uses it to justify skill files as a vehicle for motion, interaction, and design-engineering judgment rather than as magic taste transfer.

## Distilled principles

1. Taste is often articulable logic, not only personal intuition.
2. If a design decision can be explained, it can often be taught to an agent as a rule, example, or reference.
3. Package design judgment by aspect rather than as one vague mega-prompt.
4. Motion, interaction, spacing, and component behavior can each have their own skill files.
5. Reuse matters. Once a decision is codified, the team stops re-explaining it every session.
6. A skill is leverage only when it changes output or review quality.
7. Agent taste work should remain tied to real design craft, not just trend lists.
8. Human judgment still chooses what is worth encoding.
9. Sponsorship or endorsement does not make two projects the same project.
10. Parallel practitioner work can corroborate the premise that taste guidance is becoming infrastructure.
11. Skill files are a way to transmit design reasoning across tools that understand the SKILL.md convention.
12. Taste packaging should be specific enough to affect behavior, but modular enough to avoid bloating every task.
