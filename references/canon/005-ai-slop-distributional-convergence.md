## Why canonical

This coverage gives Gogh its theory of the enemy: AI slop is not random ugliness, but convergence toward high-probability design defaults. It explains why vague prompts like "make it beautiful" fail and why constraints, bans, references, and committed directions work better. Gogh uses it to connect the six skills into one shared anti-mean strategy.

## Distilled principles

1. Unguided LLM frontend output drifts toward common training-data patterns.
2. The slop signature is recognizable: neutral sans type, purple or blue gradients, centered heroes, rounded nested cards, minimal motion, and generic icons.
3. A default can be popular because it is statistically common, not because it fits the brief.
4. The purple-gradient habit plausibly comes from repeated tutorial and demo patterns, including Tailwind-style examples.
5. Asking for beauty is too high altitude; the model needs constraints that narrow the sample space.
6. Forbidden patterns work because they remove high-probability but low-fit choices.
7. Positive direction still matters. A ban list without a target aesthetic can produce empty restraint.
8. References, one chosen direction, and explicit design reads reduce median collapse.
9. Design dials act like coarse control axes over variance, motion, and density.
10. Image-first work narrows implementation to matching a concrete target instead of inventing from text alone.
11. Pre-flight checks turn taste from after-the-fact critique into in-loop review.
12. Reused skills matter because anti-slop knowledge must travel across agents and sessions.
13. The same convergence problem appears in prose patterns, not only visual UI.
14. The remedy is not novelty for its own sake. The remedy is fit: defaults must be chosen only when the brief supports them.
